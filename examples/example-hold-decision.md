# Example Hold Decision

This example demonstrates a gate card recording a HOLD decision. It shows what a well-formed hold looks like: specific condition, clear rationale, explicit path to clearing the hold.

---

**Gate ID:** GATE-EXAMPLE-002-S5  
**Cycle ID:** CYCLE-EXAMPLE-002  
**Station:** 5 — Run review  
**Date:** 2024-04-02  
**Evaluated by:** AI assistant — subject to human review

---

## Gate condition

Does the research note meet the criteria for coherence, completeness and fitness for its stated purpose (a structured research note suitable for internal reference)?

---

## Evidence evaluated

The research note was read in full. It contains four sections:

1. Context: clear and appropriately scoped.
2. Observation: present but underdeveloped; the central finding is stated but not explained.
3. Implications: absent. The section heading exists but the content is missing.
4. Open questions: two questions listed; both are relevant.

The note has a clear purpose and a usable structure. However, the central finding in Section 2 is not developed sufficiently to be useful for reference, and Section 3 is entirely missing.

---

## Decision

**Gate decision:** HOLD

---

## Rationale

The research note cannot pass the review station because:

1. The central finding (Section 2) is stated but not explained. A reader unfamiliar with the context cannot determine what the finding means or why it matters.
2. Section 3 (Implications) is missing. The note therefore cannot fulfill its stated purpose as a structured research note suitable for internal reference.

These are substantive gaps, not stylistic issues. They require the author's attention, not editorial correction.

The note has genuine value in its current form — the context is well-scoped and the open questions are well-chosen — but it cannot proceed past the review station until the gaps are addressed.

---

## Next action

The research note is on HOLD at Station 5.

To clear the hold, the author should:

1. Expand Section 2 to explain the central finding in two to four sentences. What is it? Why does it matter? What does it change?
2. Write Section 3 (Implications): describe two to three concrete implications of the finding.

When both conditions are addressed, a new cycle may be run. The new cycle will re-evaluate Station 5.

No other stations need to be re-evaluated unless the revisions change the material substantially.

**Decision overridden:** NO
