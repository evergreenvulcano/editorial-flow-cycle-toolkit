## Pattern or method

**Name:** General framing → specific constraint

**Description:**

When opening an argumentative section, begin with a question that frames the general territory being examined ("What does it mean to do X in a context where Y is the norm?"), then immediately follow with a sentence that identifies a specific operational constraint that the rest of the section will address ("The constraint is Z: it limits what X can mean in practice").

This two-move structure — framing question, then constraint — has several advantages:

- It signals to the reader that the section has a defined scope.
- It positions the argument within a recognizable territory before narrowing.
- It creates a natural anchor for the section's conclusion, which can return to the framing question and show how the constraint has been resolved or clarified.

The pattern works in expository essay writing and in structured research note formats. It is most effective when the framing question is genuinely open (not rhetorical) and when the constraint is specific enough to be operationally meaningful.

---

## Where this applies

- Argumentative essays with multiple distinct sections
- Research notes with observation sections that need to establish scope before detail
- Design memos where the context-before-constraint structure is appropriate
- Any document where a section needs to do two things: position itself in a larger territory, and commit to a specific operational focus

---

## How to apply it

1. Write the framing question first. It should be open, not rhetorical. The section should be able to address it.
2. Follow immediately with a constraint sentence. "The constraint is…" or "The operative limit here is…" or "What matters in this context is specifically…"
3. Let the rest of the section develop the constraint.
4. Close the section by returning to the framing question — not to answer it definitively, but to show what has been clarified.

The pattern is a structural guide, not a formula. Adapt the language to the register and audience of the document.

---

## Conditions and constraints

- Do not use this pattern if the framing question is rhetorical. A rhetorical question does not open territory; it closes it. The pattern requires genuine openness at the start.
- Do not use this pattern if the section has only one point to make. The two-move structure implies a relationship between territory and constraint; if there is no real constraint, the pattern adds false complexity.
- The pattern is for section-level structure. It is not appropriate for paragraph-level structure in most contexts.
